Orchestration & scheduling

Plans describe the work. Orchestration runs it — dispatching tasks to fresh AI sessions under human-gated supervision. Scheduling decides when: from always-on capture to an overnight batch you wake up to. This page covers how both are set up and governed, and is honest about what's turnkey versus what you assemble.

A deep page. The orchestrator, its gates, programmatic sessions, and the real scheduling surface.

What orchestration is

The orchestrator is a propose-and-gate dispatcher, not a fully autonomous background process. Nothing fires without a human reasoning or approval point. It reads a plan's machine-readable manifest as its contract, walks the tasks, and dispatches each one to its own fresh AI session — but it never rewrites the plan's own files.

dispatchYou point it at a plan. It resolves the manifest and the task order.
→
plan-runnerWalks the tasks in dependency order and applies a gate to each (below).
→
job-runnerRuns one task as a fresh, non-interactive session under a timeout watchdog.
→
checkA snapshot check after each task catches the failure where a task reports success but nothing actually changed.

It keeps its own state outside your vault — an execution queue, a verdict log, and a circuit-breaker that halts new dispatches after two silent failures in a row. The plan's spec, tasks, handoff, research, and decisions are left untouched; status transitions are driven by you, the hooks, and the harness, not by the dispatcher.

The two gates

Every task is sorted into one of two supervision modes before it runs. This is the safety valve that lets a dispatcher run unattended work without it running away.

HITL — human in the loop

Halts for approval

Anything irreversible or flagged as a live mutation stops and waits for an explicit approval signal. By design, a scheduled, unattended run of a live-mutation plan will simply halt rather than proceed on its own.

HOTL — human on the loop

Proceeds under watch

Reversible work proposes what it's about to do and proceeds under supervision. The human-in-the-loop gate and the circuit-breaker are the backstops if something goes wrong.

Interactive vs. programmatic sessions

Most of your work is an interactive session — you, typing, watching. Orchestrated and scheduled work instead runs in programmatic sessions: a single non-interactive claude -p ("print mode") run that executes one instruction and exits, under a watchdog. The session is told how it was invoked — on-demand versus scheduled — so it can behave appropriately. This is the same engine whether a human kicked it off or a timer did.

A naming caution

Claude Code itself also offers scheduled "routines" (its /schedule and /loop features). Those are the harness's cloud-scheduling surface, not brain-stem. This page is about brain-stem's own scheduling — which runs locally through macOS, described next.

How scheduling actually works

brain-stem's scheduler is macOS launchd — there is no cron or systemd fallback, and no single "schedule this for me" command. Think of it as two things: a handful of built-in scheduling actions, and an open path for recurring automation you wire together yourself:

What you wantHow it's doneStatus
Always-on capture (the writer pipeline) The two foundation jobs — the writer-reconciler and the doc-amender — ship as launchd templates with a renderer; you render and bootstrap them as launchd jobs in a documented post-install step (not automatically at onboarding). You bootstrap it
Run a plan or a task once at a future time A one-off delayed dispatch; a delay of more than a few hours installs itself as a one-shot launchd job that fires once at the target time. Shipped
Run anything else on a recurring schedule Hand-author a launchd job that calls the dispatcher. There's no shipped recurring scheduler beyond the two capture jobs. You assemble it

So the honest shape is: capture runs itself once you bootstrap its two launchd jobs, one-off future runs are a built-in, and recurring automation of arbitrary skills or plans is something you set up with a small launchd job. Everything here is macOS-only.

The through-line

Scheduling plus the orchestrator plus the vault-writer system is how a day's worth of routine work — capturing information, auditing the system, advancing a plan — happens without you driving each step. Start with the turnkey pieces, then assemble the recurring lifecycles that fit how you actually work.
