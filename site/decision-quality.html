The decision-quality protocol

Some moments in a session are forks in the road — a real choice between substantive paths. Left alone, the assistant tends to present the first options it thinks of, and you pick from a list it never pressure-tested. The decision-quality protocol catches those moments and insists the research happens before the question is asked.

A decision-time check on the moment the assistant poses a choice — advisory today, with a stricter mode held in reserve.

The problem it solves

It targets a specific failure mode: good intentions don't survive. A standing instruction like "always research the alternatives before recommending" degrades over a handful of sessions — the assistant means to, then drifts. Empirically, when the options are presented first, they tend to stick, even when a proper research pass would have produced a better path. The fix has to fire at the decision point, not live in a rule that rots.

How it functions in a session

The check rides on the one tool the assistant uses to ask you a structured question. When that tool fires, a guard inspects the question's shape:

The research pass it's asking for

The nudge isn't "think harder" — it's a concrete checklist to run before publishing options on a genuine fork:

  • Re-read the goals. The right answer is often already constrained by the active plan's spec and recent decisions — surface those first.
  • Check the neighbors. Adjacent plans and shared infrastructure can change the shape of the decision.
  • Read the live state, not memory. The files on disk are authoritative; a remembered fact may be stale.
  • Add one option nobody listed. The load-bearing step — generate a path not already on the table, so the protocol expands the option space rather than rationalizing the first list.

The recommendation may still be one of the originals — but it has to survive that pass.

Advisory now, blocking held in reserve

Today the protocol is Phase 1 — advisory: it nudges and lets the question through. A stricter Phase 2 — blocking exists but is opt-in, not the default: it would refuse a substantive question until it's annotated as research-complete. Shipping advisory-first, with telemetry, is deliberate — the blocking mode earns its promotion only once the data shows the heuristic fires cleanly.

Why it lives in the sessions section

Checkpoints keep a session's thread alive; coordination keeps sessions out of each other's way; this keeps a session's decisions honest. It's the third kind of session-management capability — on-demand, firing exactly when the work reaches a fork — and like the others it's structural: a hook at the decision point, not a hope that good intentions hold.

Reference

  • The guard — pre-asq-guard.sh, a decision-time check on the structured-question tool; a substance heuristic (option count + description length, or decision keywords) with yes/no and annotated questions skipped.
  • The annotation — a research_complete: marker is the signal that the pass has been run; it lets a substantive question through (and, in the blocking mode, is required).
  • Phases — Phase 1 (advisory nudge) is shipped and on by default; Phase 2 (blocking) is opt-in via an environment flag.
  • Telemetry — every firing appends to decision-quality-events.jsonl; successful passes can surface in a plan's handoff chronicle.
