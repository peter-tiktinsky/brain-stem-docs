Plans

A plan is how a multi-step effort is stored on disk and shepherded from a raw idea to a finished, verified, signed-off result. The whole tree lives in ~/.claude-plans, a deliberate parallel to Claude Code's own plans folder, with a structure chosen against researched best practices. That one relocation is what makes the funnel, the binder, and the orchestrator possible.

Surfaced in the vault as Plans/; rolled up per project into Projects/.

Why a parallel plan tree — and why it's concrete

Claude Code treats its own config folder (~/.claude/**) as sensitive and guards it — writes and edits there fail unreliably. A plan tree has to be written to constantly: re-rendering tasks.md, regenerating indexes, scrubbing research into the Library. A store you cannot reliably write to is not a store. The vault itself is no good either — files in a sync boundary fail silently. So the tree lives outside the guarded folder, at ~/.claude-plans, and a single Plans/ symlink makes the whole thing browsable in your notes app.

This is not an ad-hoc convention. brain-stem researched best practices in plan organization and execution and adopted a structure that converges with the field's own answers (the evidence is in the next section). Native Claude Code contributes almost nothing here on its own — no enforced quartet, no lifecycle, no source-of-truth control file, no funnel, no binder, no orchestrator. Plans is the one pillar brain-stem adopts as a methodology rather than receives as a platform feature, and the parallel folder is what gives that methodology somewhere to live.

The same folder setup powers two consolidations

Because the plan tree is a writable, governed, plain-text store, brain-stem can roll it up two ways at once. Per-project binders (Projects/) consolidate one project's context across all its plans. And the one store Claude maintains universally across every project — the Library (Wiki/) — consolidates reusable context promoted out of those plans. (There is no separate "universal binder"; the universal store is the Library.) Binders for project context, the Library for universal context, both derived from the same tree.

The quartet — and why it's best-practice

Each plan is a numbered folder holding four files with sharply separated jobs (plus an optional fifth, the ideation brief — the "Session 0" research record).

FileJobOwner
spec.mdThe goal and constraints — the north star. Fixed once set.Human-authored
manifest.jsonThe source of truth — status plus the structured task list, schema-validated.Machine-validated
tasks.mdThe human-readable to-do — a generated render of the manifest, between sentinel markers.Machine-rendered
handoff.mdAppend-only, newest-first session diary; its Next session: line carries the resume.Appended per session
The manifest is the spreadsheet; tasks.md is its export

Because tasks.md is generated from the manifest between sentinel markers, hand-edits inside those markers are overwritten on the next render. Change status in the manifest; read it in tasks.md. As the design doc puts it: done is recorded in the spreadsheet, not penciled onto the printout.

Why this shape — the evidence

None of this is a private invention, which is the strongest signal it's right. The forgeable-checkbox failure (an agent claiming it ticked a box it didn't) and the up-only reporting rule both track the documented failure modes of unsupervised multi-agent systems (a published multi-agent failure taxonomy). The completed-versus-verified distinction is standard project-management practice (the PMI / PMBOK separation of verified from accepted deliverables). And a structured source of truth rendered into a human list — not the reverse — is the lesson agentic-coding tools settled on independently (the tasks.json-style pattern). The design converges with those external answers rather than departing from them.

Beyond the quartet: research and decisions

A plan folder gives the quartet two governed companions — used both before execution (during planning) and during execution at human gates:

  • Research — _research/. Durable findings, each declared in the manifest's research_artifacts[] (with a status of active / finalized / deferred). A finalized finding is the stock the Library promotes from, and every finding rolls into the binder's research index.
  • Decisions — the ADR ledger. A per-plan decisions/ folder of immutable decision records (each Context · Decision · Consequences), declared in decision_records[]. A decision never changes in place — a new ADR supersedes the old one, forward-linked. On resume, Claude reloads the non-superseded ADRs straight from the manifest pointer, not from spec archaeology.

Not every idea deserves a numbered plan on day one. The funnel keeps capture cheap and spends structure only on ideas that earn it. A number is never burned until graduation.

Capture & triage — an idea note (/backlog-triage) is classified against what already exists, so duplicates and overlaps are caught before they become plans.
Research-first — /backlog-research reads your vault, your tools, and outside best practices, then writes the ideation brief and a recommendation.

Ideas carry their own short status — new → triaged → briefed — a separate vocabulary from a plan's lifecycle. The backlog itself is generated and owned by the librarian: the funnel skills only edit the underlying idea notes, and the table is re-derived from them. The human gates here are the classification call at triage and the go/no-go on the research recommendation.

Stage two — preparing the plan

A freshly graduated plan is scaffolded but thin. Preparation is where you and Claude flesh it out, and the file ownership stays strict the whole way:

  • You author the spec — the goal and constraints, fixed once set.
  • Tasks live in the manifest — you expand the structured task list there; the machine regenerates tasks.md from it between the sentinels (your edits outside the sentinels are preserved).
  • Each session appends a handoff — newest-first, carrying the resume pointer.
  • Research and decisions accrue beside the quartet — feasibility research lands in _research/ and planning decisions in the ADR ledger, both declared in the manifest, both well before any code is written.

The plan moves through its early states as this settles: researching → planned. When the spec is firm and the tasks are real, it's ready to execute — the human gate of plan readiness.

Earning done — completed, verified, closed

The lifecycle's load-bearing guard is the difference between a claim and a fact. Verification is not a checkbox — it's an independent pass: a separate, read-only verifier returns a pass/fail verdict, and the status is stamped verified only when that real check confirms the work.

in-progress
›
completeda human claim
›
verifiedmachine-stamped by a real check — never typed in

paused ⇄ in-progress — work can pause (with a reason) and resume without losing its place.

Completed ≠ verified ≠ closed

Completed is a human claim. Verified is stamped only by a real validation pass — never typed in by a person. Closed is structurally refused at write-time unless the plan is already verified. Done is not something you get to declare. (Archiving is later still, driven by librarian volume, not a calendar date.)

Flat plans and master plans

Most plans are flat: one numbered folder, one quartet, one status — the right shape for a single coherent effort you can hold in one spec. Flat is the default, and a flat plan never silently grows into something bigger; you opt into more structure explicitly, only when you need it.

When an effort is too large for one spec — several workstreams or phases that each deserve their own goal, tasks, and tracking, but that have to be coordinated — it becomes a master plan that coordinates a set of sub-plans. The difference is real, not cosmetic:

The default

A flat plan

One quartet, one lifecycle status. The whole effort lives in a single spec and task list. Reach for it whenever the work fits in one coherent plan — which is most of the time.

Opt-in, for scale

A master + sub-plans

A coordinating master, plus sub-plans that are each a first-class plan — own quartet, own 8-state status, numbered in execution order. Reach for it when one spec can't honestly hold the whole effort.

On disk, a master is a plan whose folder contains its sub-plans, each a full plan in its own right:

~/.claude-plans/
└── 42-rebuild-search/            the MASTER (type: master) — its own quartet
    ├── spec.md  manifest.json  tasks.md  handoff.md
    ├── 01-index-schema/          sub-plan 01 (type: sub-plan) — its own quartet
    ├── 02-query-rewrite/         sub-plan 02 — runs after 01 (ordering held by the master)
    └── 03-ranking/               sub-plan 03
                                  └ sub-plans numbered in EXECUTION order, not creation order

The invariants that keep it honest

The structure earns its keep through three strict rules — the same orchestrator-worker discipline the multi-agent research points to as what keeps decomposed work from collapsing:

  • Sub-plans never talk to each other. A sub-plan carries no dependency edge to a sibling; any cross-sub ordering is held by the master. (No sibling chatter — the coupling that sinks unsupervised multi-agent systems.)
  • Subs publish status upward only. Each sub owns and updates its own status; it never reaches into the master. The master pulls, never the reverse.
  • The master's roll-up is generated, never hand-typed. The master's summary of its sub-plans is a derived read-replica, regenerated by the librarian from each sub's real published status — the same trust class as a generated index. You cannot pencil in a sub's progress on the master.
Why it exists — and why it's exactly what the orchestrator walks

A master plan is the orchestrator-worker pattern made structural: a big effort decomposed into independently-governed pieces, coordinated from one place, with no peer-to-peer coupling. That isn't just tidy — it's executable. Because the master holds the dependency ordering and each sub is a real plan with real tasks, the orchestrator can walk a master as a dependency graph, running its sub-plans in execution order behind the same human gates. A flat plan gives you one tracked effort; a master gives you a coordinated, gate-able program of them — which is why the structure exists at all, and why you only pay for it when the scale demands it.
