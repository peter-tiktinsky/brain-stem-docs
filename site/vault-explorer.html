The one idea to hold first

On a fresh install brain-stem seeds almost nothing: one guide file (CLAUDE.md), one real folder (Vault Writers/), an .obsidian/ config, and five shortcuts. A shortcut (technically a symlink) is a signpost to a folder that lives elsewhere on disk — it shows up here for easy browsing, but the real files stay in their own home, so deleting the vault never touches your plans, projects, or Library. The vault arrives nearly empty and is grown over time. And you never start a session from the vault root — you always launch from a project folder (a code repo, or a project under Work).

Who creates what — read the colors this way

Almost nothing in your brain is purely hand-typed by you. brain-stem lays down the structure at install, and from there you provide the direction and the raw inputs while Claude generates the artifacts — filing each one in its proper place, under governance and curation, and keeping the derived pages current. So read the colors as where a piece mostly comes from, not as solo authorship; the honest rule of thumb is you steer, Claude builds and maintains. Green marks the files your direction and inputs drive (brain-stem scaffolds the shell and Claude fills it with you); red marks the pages Claude generates and refreshes on its own, which you never hand-edit.

What the brain folder is for

It keeps your context layer readable to you. Claude stores everything it captures as plain Markdown, so the whole store stays human-readable and navigable — no database, no locked format. Obsidian is simply the interface that makes it pleasant to browse: it opens the folder as a vault, lets you read, search, and organize its contents, and draws a knowledge graph that visualizes your assets and the relationships between them. It is optional — any editor opens the files — but it is the intended window onto your brain.

It consolidates your entire context layer into one canonical location. Everything relevant to your Claude Code work — your plans, the Library, project binders, your work area — actually lives in its own optimal home on disk; the brain folder gathers all of it in one place through the symlinks marked above. So each artifact stays where it belongs, yet the whole of your context is reviewable from a single spot. That canonical home doesn't only serve you: it lets Claude Code — or any other LLM harness you point at it — use your accumulated context as reference material. If you ever multi-home, it is the feeder for a local LLM that turns your knowledge base into a queryable asset, or for a vector-search layer that gives you another way to navigate and query everything you've built.
